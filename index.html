Shooting it with a DWARF 3 ›

A transit is over in about a second, so the whole job is preparation: lock focus and tracking on the disk first, then roll high-frame-rate video across the predicted moment and pull the sharp frame afterwards. A single still can't be timed this finely.

Tripod. Any photo tripod (¼″-20 thread). Get it genuinely steady and don't touch it once tracking has started — there's no time to recompose mid-pass. EQ polar-alignment isn't needed for a one-second event; ordinary alt-az tracking is fine.

Solar filter — Sol only. Clip the included magnetic ND solar filter on before the lens ever faces the Sun, and leave it there: it protects the sensor and is the only route to a usable solar exposure. For a Lunar transit, shoot the bare lens — no filter.

Find, focus, track. Enter Solar System Mode and select Sol or Lunar (the app labels them Sun / Moon). Centre it with the wide lens, run Auto Focus on the tele view, then fine-tune focus by hand on a sunspot or a crater edge. Begin tracking so the disk stays put. Sharp focus matters more than any other setting here.

Skip the default auto-capture. The built-in Sun/Moon routine picks a slow ~1/250 s exposure tuned for surface detail — at that speed the station smears across the disk. Switch to Video mode (or a fast burst), where you can set shutter and gain yourself.

Shutter — fast. Roughly 1/1000–1/2000 s to freeze the ISS. Much slower than about 1/500 s and the silhouette blurs out.

Gain — lift to compensate. A fast shutter needs more gain; judge it by the live preview — disk bright but not clipped to white. Through the solar filter, and on a daytime Lunar disk, expect a healthy bump; a Lunar pass at night needs far less. Keep the VIS (visual) internal filter for both.

2K at 60 fps, not 4K. More frames across the ~1-second window means more chances at a crisp station — that beats extra resolution. The disk is small in this scope's 2.9° × 1.7° field anyway, so plan to crop in afterwards.

Roll early. Start recording 10–15 seconds before the listed time — clock and orbit each carry a little slack — and keep going a few seconds past it. Check your phone's clock is accurate; the times on each card are to the second, in both local time and UTC.

Afterwards. Scrub the clip for the frames holding the silhouette, then stack and sharpen them in Stellar Studio (or PIPP / AutoStakkert). And since the visible path is only a few kilometres wide, re-check the prediction the morning of and confirm the centre-line on transit-finder.com before travelling.

Method & accuracy ›

How this works. It pulls the ISS's current orbital elements (TLE) and propagates the orbit with the SGP4 model, second by second, through every daytime pass over your coordinates. At each step it compares the station's direction with the Sun's and reports the moments they come closest. A transit happens when that separation drops below the Sun's angular radius (about 16 arcminutes).

Be in the right place. A transit is only visible from a path on the ground a few kilometres wide. These times are computed for the exact coordinates above — move a few kilometres and a "near miss" becomes a hit, or vice-versa. Before travelling for a shot, confirm the precise centre-line with a dedicated tool such as transit-finder.com.

Freshness matters. TLE data drifts; predictions are reliable for roughly a week and degrade after that. Re-run this with a fresh orbit a day or two before any event you plan to observe. Times near the horizon are the least certain, as atmospheric refraction and terrain come into play.

What it doesn't do. It doesn't apply atmospheric refraction or solar-disk weighting, and it treats the Sun's position geocentrically (an ~9-arcsecond simplification). For planning it's accurate to a second or two; for a once-in-months photograph, verify against transit-finder and re-check the morning of.
